• Dayerye A herd of milk (milche in Middle English) cows; a dairy.

    • "His lordes sheep, his neet, his dayerye" - Prologue to The Canterbury Tales

    "Thropes, bernes, shipnes, dayeryes" - The Wife of Bath's Tale

    "Thropes" are villages, "bernes" are barns, and "shipnes" are cowsheds.

  • Deer Deer; not just the woodland mammal of the family Cervidae, with hoofs and antlers, but any wild animal. The Old English version, deor, meant beast, and the Middle English deer meant both deer and animal. Chaucer and his contemporaries were usually more specific when referring directly to deer, and used the classifications of stag, doe, buck, etc. (Thus indicating a closer relationship with this animal than modern society has.)

    • "That kan an hurt deer from an hool knowe" - The Friar's Tale

    "He koude hunte at wilde deer" - Sir Thopas

    For more information on the popularity of deer in Medieval thought and culture, please visit: In the Pursuit of Venison.

  • Deye A dairywoman. Chaucer's widow in the Nun's Priest's Tale was a dairywoman on a manor, whose duties included looking after livestock.

    • "For she was, as it were, a maner deye" - The Nun's Priest's Tale

  • Drake Drake; a male duck. Ducks and other waterfowl were, after pheasant, the best and most preferred wild birds. When cooking duck, the 14th c. cook Chiquart would quote from a Latin couplet he learned as an apprentice:

    • "Si volucris verrat, qui torret eam, procul errat; Sed procul ab igne volucrem de flumine torre."
    "If the bird is at home on the land, he who roasts it will go far; but if it is a water bird, it should be roasted far from the fire."

    "The drake, stroyere of his owene kynde" - The Parlement of Foules

    Drakes were known in the Middle Ages to kill their own offspring and other young ducks.

  • Draughte A quantity or a portion drawn off; a drink. The more modern spelling would be Draft.

    • "Of grece, whan she dronken hadde hir draughte" - Prologue to The Canterbury Tales

    "Wel koude he knowe a draughte of London ale" - Prologue to The Canterbury Tales

    "Ful many a draughte of wyn had he ydrawe" - Prologue to The Canterbury Tales
