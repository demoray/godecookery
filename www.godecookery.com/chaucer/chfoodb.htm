• Bacon, Bacoun Bacon, less like our modern variety and more like cured ham; pork; smoked pork; a side of pork; not fresh meat. Its use in Chaucer is mainly with those who can't afford or find fresh meat.

    • "Seyned bacoun, and somtyme an ey or tweye." - The Nun's Priest's Tale

    "And yet in bacon hadde I nevere delit." - The Wife of Bath's Tale

    "The bacon was nat fet for hem, I trowe." - The Wife of Bath's Tale

    "Bacon or boef, or swich thyng as ye fynde." - The Summoner's Tale

    The Wife of Bath uses "bacon" as a term for "old men," in which she never finds delight!

  • Bake Mete A pie in which meat or fish is baked with fruit, spices, etc. "Mete" is not "meat," as is easily assumed, but "meal," and such meat pies, common in the Middle Ages, contained a plethora of ingredients and were literally a meal within themselves. A pie shell with lid allowed the meat inside to bake, rather than roast, and quite frequently the pastry was considered superfluous and was discarded. Two Fifteenth-Century Cookery-Books contains an entire section devoted to Bake Metis.

    • "Withoute bake mete was nevere his hous,
    Of fissh and flessh, and that so plentvous" - Prologue to The Canterbury Tales

  • Bakere Baker. Being a baker was a distinct profession in its own right, one that was closely regulated by civic authorities who always needed to maintain concern over the size, quality, weight, and price of bread. The uncertain availability and cost of bread was always a constant worry and fluctuating bread prices caused hardship & anxiety; prices were fixed and bakers were monitored to ensure quality control. A baker found guilty of making insubstantial loaves would be pilloried or worse. The making of bread was a commercial enterprise, and bakers joined powerful guilds as one would join a union today. In 1303 the London Baker's Company succesfully obtained for its members enough allowance to cover the cost of wood, candles, journeymen and apprentices, salt, yeast, the miller's charges, the baker's house, a cat, and even a wife.

    • "His bakere and his butiller also" - The Nun's Priest's Tale

  • Barley-breed Barley Bread. Made from a course grain, it was considered appropraite fare for the working class but not for nobles or the wealthy, whose digestive sysytems were too "aristocratic" for such vulgar food.

    • "Lat hem be breed of pured whete seed,
    And lat us wyves hoten barly-breed;
    And yet with barly-breed, Mark telle kan,
    Oure Lord Jhesu refresshed many a man" - The Wife of Bath's Tale

    The reference here is to the Miracle of the Loaves & Fishes.

  • Barmcloth An apron; barm means bosom or lap.

    • "A barmcloth eek as whit as morne milk" - The Miller's Tale

  • Barel Ale A barrel of ale.

    • "I hadde levere than a barel ale" - Prologue to The Monk's Tale

  • Bee; Been Bee; bees. Bee-keepers performed a valuable activity and their beehives were home to enormous swarms producing vast amounts of honey. Honey was one of the premium sweeteners in the Middle Ages.

    • "Lo, lyk a bisy bee, withouten gile" - The Second Nun's Tale

    "That ferde as been don in an hive" - The Hous of Fame

    "For nevere yet so thikke a swarm of been" - Troilus and Criseyde

    "Out of the hyve cam the swarm of bees" - The Nun's Priest's Tale

    "Right so as bees out swarmen from an hyve" - The Summoner's Prolouge

  • Bene Bean. Beans were among the vegetables and fruits that were commonly served at meal times, in a variety of ways: boiled, baked, mashed, etc. Le Menagier de Paris advises that their preparation is simple and most cooks are aware of how to cook them, and so recipes for beans are rare in period manuscripts.

    • "She preyseth nat his pleyyng worth a bene" - The Merchant's Tale

  • Bene-straw A bean straw. An inedible part of the bean plant.

    • "It is but bene-straw and greet forage" - The Merchant's Tale

  • Berme Yeast. Yeast was the primary leavening agent used in making ale, beer, mead, & other fermented drinks, in addition to breads, pastries, and other raised dough products.

    • "Of tartre, alum glas, berme, wort, and argoille" - The Canon's Yeoman's Tale

  • Bitore A bittern; a heron. A bittern is a long-necked, brownish, wading-bird with a resonant cry. Its Latin name, butitauras, means "bird that bellows like an ox." Heron were often consumed in the Middle Ages and heron recipes can be found in period manuscripts. Generally, they were roasted, with an accompanying sauce to prevent dryness.

    • "And as a bitore bombleth in the myre" - The Wife of Bath's Tale

  • Blankmanger Blancmange. Not the pudding so popular in England today, but a dish of rice boiled in almond milk, often with chunks of chicken or sometimes fish. It's name means "white food." Chaucer's cook was proficient in making this popular dish and a variety of versions were in existence.

    • "For blankmanger, that made he with the beste" - Prologue to The Canterbury Tales

  • Bocher Butcher. Domestic meat made up a good portion of the bourgeois diet during the late Middle Ages, and butchers were kept busy throughout the year, except during Lent. Like the baker, their industry was highly regulated as the Medieval population insisted upon good, fresh, healthy meat, properly cut and at a fair weight and price. Regulations concerning the sale of good meat were strictly enforced with harsh punishments. Professional guilds levied fines on members, and strict public officials exacted severe punishments to offending butchers. In London, butchers who sold meat that was "putrid and stinking, an abomination to mankind;" "putrid, rotten, stinking, and abominable to the human race;" and "putrid and corrupt, unwholesome as food for man, and an abomination" were "without exception punished... by being placed in the pillory and having the putrid matter burnt beneath." Equally regulated was the disposal of the butchering wastes, which created stench and disease in the closely packed cities. The descriptions of how slaughterhouses disposed of large amounts of blood, hair, dung, and entrails would be amusing if not for the chilling glimpse they provide of the reality of food production in the Middle Ages.

    • The barbour, and the bocher, and the smyth" - The Knight's Tale

  • Boef Beef; the meat of oxen and cows. Domestic meats were eaten in moderately generous amounts in Medieval towns, and according to slaughterhouse records, beef was the most popular meat between the months of September and February.

    • "Bacon or boef, or swich thyng as ye fynde." - The Summoner's Tale

    "And bet than old boef is the tendre veel" - The Merchant's Tale

  • Boor Boar, an uncastrated male pig; a wild pig with dense, dark bristles. The meat of the wild pig was preferred over domestic pork. The belief was that game animals were active, and boar would therefore provide a healthier meat than domestic swine.

    • "He groneth lyk oure boor, lith in oure sty" - The Summoner's Tale

    "He slow the grisly boor, and that anon" - The Monk's Tale

  • Bores Heed Boar's Head. The roasted head of a boar, decorated for the occasion, was a regular feature of feasts during Christmastide & 12th Night, and was presented at other times of the year as well. A boar's head doesn't provide the tastiest of meat but was prized because of the skill required in hunting the beast.

    • "And therinne was a bores heed" - Sir Thopas

  • Bragot A drink made of ale and honey fermented together; a spiced ale drink, sweetened with honey. Receipts are found in Forme of Cury (as braggot) and in Goud Kokery (as brakkat). Judging by the prices paid by Alice de Bryene in 1419, the spice quantities would be 2/3 oz. pepper & 1/4 oz. cloves.

    • "Hir mouth was sweete as bragot or the meeth" - The Miller's Tale

  • Brawen The flesh of the boar or the "tusked swine;" the dark meat of pork or chicken. The word is Germanic in origin and is related to the Old English word for flesh, "braed." Two 15th-Century Cookery-Books lists recipes where "braun" is most certainly boar meat, or the dark meat of pork: "Braun en peueruade" is translated as "Boar in egerdouce." Le Viandier de Taillevent defines "braun" as the dark meat of poultry; however, the author of Curye on Inglish says that "braun" of chicken is the white, or breast portion, of the meat.

    • "Or yif us of youre brawen, if ye have eny" - The Summoner's Tale

    "Biforn hym stant brawen of the tusked swine'" - The Franklin's Tale

  • Brede Roast meat.

    • "That ben at festes with the brede" - The House of Fame

  • Breed, Bred Bread. One of the most common and vital foodstuffs of the Middle Ages, it was, and has always been a daily staple of life. Essential to Medieval society, a rise in the cost of wheat or a scarcity of bread usually marked the beginning of a time of famine or economic calamity. Figures indicate that in England, the average lorly household allowed everyone about 2 to 3 pounds of wheat bread a day, while in France wheat records show that each citizen had enough wheat for about a 2-pound loaf each day. Clearly, bread was the basis of the Medieval diet. References to many varieties of bread appear throughout Chaucer's writings.

    • "And of youre softe breed nat but a shyvere" - The Summoner's Tale

    "Certes, I nil never ete breed" - The Book of the Duchess

    "Fro this forth I shal nevere eten bred" - Troilus and Criseyde

    "And brynge us breed and wyn ful prively" - The Pardoner's Tale

  • Breed ne Ale Bread nor Ale; used in an expression which meant to "not stop."

    • "He foond neither to selle
    "Ne breed ne ale, til he cam to the celle" - The Miller's Tale

    This would translate as, "He didn't stop, until he came to the cellar." An Old French Fabliaux contains two instances of the same expression.

  • Breem A Bream, the European freshwater Cyprinid or related fishes; a Porgy, or any of various freshwater Sunfishes, especially the Bluegill. Recipes for Bream appear in Two Fifteenth-Century Cookery-Books, Forme of Curye, etc. Bream were among the fish that were domestically raised specifically for food, and are found in the Franklin's stocked fish pond, or "stuwe."

    • "And many a breem and many a luce in stuwe" - Prologue to The Canterbury Tales

  • Bren Bran; the broken coats of individual seeds of cereal grain, separated from the flour or meal by sifting or bolting.

    • "In stide of flour yet wol I yeve hem bren" - The Reeve's Tale

    "The bren, as I best kan, now most I selle" - The Wife of Bath's Tale

    "But I ne kan nat bulte it to the bren" - The Nun's Priest's Tale

  • Brewhous A Brewhouse; an Alehouse.

    • "In al the toun nas brewhous ne taverne" - The Miller's Tale

  • Broun breed Brown bread. Not considered as fine as white, brown bread was usually reserved for the lower classes and the poor, such as the widow in the Nun's Priest's Tale.

    • "Milk and broun breed, in which she foond no lak" - The Nun's Priest's Tale

  • Bryd, Brid(d), Bryddes, Briddes Birds; more specifically in cooking, the smaller birds, such as swallows, hummingbirds, larks, turtledoves, pigeons, etc. Medieval man ate practically anything with wings, and small birds were prepared in a number of ways, often being used to stuff larger birds or other animals as an added treat for the diner. The frequency with which such fowl appear in period cookbooks indicates their popularity, especially in pies ("four and-twenty blackbirds..."). In Medieval times, there were four classes of birds, which symbolized the four classes of society: birds of prey/nobles, birds that eat worms/bourgeois class, water-fowl/merchant class, & seed-fowl/agricultural class. Birds are the primary characters in Chaucer's The Parlement of Foules, a fable about how birds choose their mates on St. Valentine's Day, a popular Medieval belief.

    • "Bridd, fissh, best, or him or here" - The Hous of Fame

    "As man, brid, best, fissh, herbe, and grene tree" - Troilus and Criseyde

    "My faire bryd, my sweete cynamome" - The Miller's Tale

    "On every bow the bryddes herde I synge" - The Parlement of Foules

  • Bugle Horn A drinking vessel made from the horn of a bugle (a buffalo) or wild ox.

    • "And drynketh of his bugle horn the wyn" - The Franklin's Tale

  • Buk, Bukkes A male deer of at least 6 years of age. The Book of St. Alban (1486) states that a buck is called a fawn in its first year, a sore in its fourth, and a buck in its sixth.

    • "The dredful ro, the buk, the hert and hynde" - The Parlement of Foules

    "Ye, bothe bukke and hare" - Sir Thopas

    "Of founes, sowres, bukkes, does" - The Book of the Duchess
