• Ellebor The herb Hellebore (Helleborus), used as a laxative and to purge "melancholy." Also called Christe Herbe, it was once used to bless cattle and keep them away from evil spells. In an old French Romance, a wizard, when passing through an enemy's camp, makes himself invisible by scattering powdered Hellebore in the air.

    • "Or elles of ellebor, that groweth there" - The Nun's Priest's Tale

  • Entremes A dish served between the courses of an elaborate meal. Spelled "entremets" in most period cookbooks, this simple little filler presented between the larger courses of a meal started out as a pause where the diner's palate could take a break from the rich and delightful foods had elsewhere in the feast; however, this simplicity was lost as the entremet eventually evolved into the elaborate subtleties that Medieval cooks are famous for, where foods are disguised and formed into fanciful shapes, creatures, and designs.

    • "This entremes is dressed for yow alle" - The Parlement of Foules

  • Ey Egg. Eggs were then, as now, a vital staple of life. Medieval folk viewed the egg as being both nutritious and easily digestible, and their constant availability made them relatively affordable. Every peasant owned chickens, and the selling of eggs gave many individuals a steady income. Because of the difficulty in keeping and storing, the frequency in which they are called for in period recipes indicates an active trade and commerce in providing fresh eggs to the market and kitchen.

    • "Seynd bacoun, and sometyme an ey or tweye" - The Nun's Priest's Tale

    "Unslekked lym, chalk, and gleyre of an ey" - The Canon's Yeoman's Tale

    The "gleyre of an ey" is the white of an egg.
